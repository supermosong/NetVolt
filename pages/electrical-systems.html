Electrical Systems

Understand the electricity that powers every device you own — from the voltage in your wall socket to the tiny circuits inside your phone's battery.

Beginner ~20 min read 5-Question Quiz

What is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electrons through a conductor — like water flowing through a pipe.

Everything is made of atoms. Atoms contain tiny particles called electrons. In certain materials — especially metals — electrons can be pushed loose and made to flow from one place to another. That flow of electrons is electric current, and it is what powers every device you own.

Three measurements describe any electrical circuit. The water-pipe analogy below makes them easy to picture:

Ohm's Law — the most important formula in electronics

V = I × R  —  Voltage (V) equals Current (I) multiplied by Resistance (R). Double the resistance and the current halves. Double the voltage and the current doubles. Every electronic component is designed around this relationship.

Voltage — V (Volts)

The electrical "pressure" that pushes electrons around a circuit. A AA battery provides 1.5 V; a UK wall socket provides 230 V. Higher voltage can push more current through the same resistance.

Current — I (Amps)

The rate at which electrons flow past a point in the circuit — like litres per second in a pipe. A phone charger draws about 1–3 A; a kettle draws around 10 A.

Resistance — R (Ohms Ω)

How much a component opposes the flow of current. Every material has some resistance — copper wire has very low resistance (good conductor); rubber has very high resistance (good insulator).

AC vs DC

There are two fundamentally different ways electric current can flow — and both power your home simultaneously.

DC (Direct Current) flows in one steady direction — like water flowing downhill. Batteries produce DC. AC (Alternating Current) reverses direction many times per second — it "alternates". Wall sockets provide AC because AC travels long distances through power lines far more efficiently than DC.

Basic Circuit Components

Every electronic device is built from a small set of fundamental components — each with one specific job.

A circuit is a closed loop that allows current to flow. Components are connected into this loop to control, shape, or convert that current into something useful — light, sound, motion, or computation.

A one-way valve for electricity — allows current to flow in one direction only and blocks it in reverse. Key component in rectifiers (converting AC to DC) and circuit protection.

Light Emitting Diode. A diode that produces light when current flows through it. Extremely efficient — uses up to 90% less energy than an incandescent bulb. Found in screens, torches, traffic lights, and indicator lamps.

Acts as an electronic switch or amplifier. A tiny current at one pin controls a much larger current. A modern CPU contains billions of transistors switching on and off to process 1s and 0s.

Opens or closes a circuit to stop or allow current flow. A light switch is the simplest example. In electronics, transistors act as microscopic switches that can toggle billions of times per second.

Series vs Parallel Circuits

Components in a circuit can be connected in two fundamentally different ways — and the difference matters enormously.

Series Circuit

All components share one single path. The same current flows through every component. If any one component breaks, the circuit is open and everything stops — like old Christmas lights where one bulb kills the whole string.

  • Same current everywhere
  • Voltage split between components
  • One failure = all fail

Parallel Circuit

Components each have their own path back to the source. Each branch receives the full supply voltage. If one branch fails, the others keep working — this is how your home wiring is designed.

  • Same voltage on each branch
  • Current splits between branches
  • One failure = others unaffected

Real-world example

Your home is wired in parallel — each socket gets the full 230 V and unplugging one device doesn't affect the others. Older fairy lights were wired in series — one blown bulb knocked out the whole string.

How Electricity Powers Technology

Every device you own takes raw electricity and transforms it into exactly the voltage and current it needs.

Wall sockets supply AC at a high voltage — too high and the wrong type for most electronics. Every device contains circuitry to convert and regulate this supply into something safe and stable for its components.

Your charger brick is a miniature power supply that does four things:

  1. Steps down voltage — a transformer reduces 230 V AC to a much lower level.
  2. Rectifies — diodes convert AC to DC.
  3. Filters — capacitors smooth out ripples in the DC.
  4. Regulates — a voltage regulator chip locks the output to exactly 5 V (USB-A) or up to 20 V (USB-C fast charging) so it matches your phone's requirements.

A PC power supply converts mains AC into multiple DC voltages that different components need:

  • 12 V — powers the CPU, GPU, and case fans
  • 5 V — powers USB ports and older storage drives
  • 3.3 V — powers RAM and some motherboard circuits

A PSU is rated in watts — e.g. a 650 W PSU can deliver up to 650 W of total power across all its outputs simultaneously. Choose a PSU with headroom above your system's peak consumption.

A lithium-ion battery stores energy as chemical potential energy. During charging, electricity forces lithium ions to move from the positive electrode to the negative electrode through a liquid electrolyte — storing energy in the process. During discharge (powering your phone), the ions flow back, releasing electrons as electrical current.

Each charge cycle slightly degrades the electrodes, which is why battery capacity slowly decreases over time. Modern phones limit charging to ~80% by default to slow this degradation.

When electricity travels through a long cable, some energy is lost as heat due to resistance. This loss is proportional to current — lower current means less loss. Transformers can only work with AC, and they can step AC up to very high voltages (reducing current for the same power). The grid transmits at 400,000 V to minimise losses over hundreds of kilometres, then transformers step it back down to 230 V at your home.

DC cannot be stepped up or down with a simple transformer — this was the key reason AC won the "War of Currents" between Nikola Tesla (AC) and Thomas Edison (DC) in the 1880s.
