Wi-Fi & Wireless

Discover how your phone, laptop, and smart TV connect to the internet without a single cable — and what's actually happening inside your router right now.

Beginner ~20 min read 5-Question Quiz

What is Wi-Fi?

Wi-Fi lets devices communicate with a router using invisible radio waves — no cables needed.

Wi-Fi stands for Wireless Fidelity. It is a technology that uses radio waves — the same family of waves used by FM radio and Bluetooth — to carry data through the air between your device and a router.

Think of it like a two-way walkie-talkie. Your laptop asks the router for a webpage; the router receives that request, fetches the page from the internet, and sends the data back — all through the air, at the speed of light, in milliseconds.

A brief history

Wi-Fi was invented in 1997 and became widespread in the early 2000s. Today it is governed by a set of technical standards called IEEE 802.11 — the version letter (a, b, g, n, ac, ax) tells you how fast and how far each generation can reach.

Radio Waves & Frequencies

Wi-Fi transmits data as radio waves. The frequency it uses controls the trade-off between speed and range.

A frequency is how many times a wave oscillates (vibrates) per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). Higher frequency means more data per second — but the waves lose energy faster and cannot pass through walls as easily.

How a Router Works

A router is the traffic controller of your home network — it decides where every packet of data goes.

When you visit a website, your request is broken into small chunks called packets. Your device sends these packets to the router wirelessly. The router then forwards them to your modem, which connects to your Internet Service Provider (ISP), which reaches the website's server. The response travels back the same path — in under a second.

Router

Manages traffic between all devices on your home network and the wider internet. Assigns each device a local IP address and decides which packets go where. Often has built-in Wi-Fi.

Modem

Translates the digital signals from your router into a format your ISP's network understands (and vice versa). It is your home's gateway to the wider internet.

ISP

Your Internet Service Provider (e.g. BT, Virgin, Comcast) owns the physical cables or fibre that link your home to the rest of the internet.

Common Questions

SSID stands for Service Set Identifier — it is simply the name of your Wi-Fi network (for example "HomeNetwork_5G"). When your device searches for Wi-Fi it shows a list of nearby SSIDs. Each router broadcasts its SSID so devices know it exists.
An IP address (Internet Protocol address) is a unique number assigned to every device on a network — like a postal address for data. Your router gives each device on your home network a local IP address (e.g. 192.168.1.5). Your whole home also has one public IP address assigned by your ISP.
DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol) is the system that automatically assigns IP addresses to devices when they join the network. When your phone connects to Wi-Fi it asks the router "what address should I use?" — DHCP answers automatically so you never have to configure it manually.

Wi-Fi Security

Without a password, anyone nearby could join your network and spy on your traffic. Security protocols encrypt your data so only authorised devices can read it.

Encryption scrambles data before it travels through the air so that even if someone intercepts it, they see meaningless gibberish. Wi-Fi security has gone through several generations — each one stronger than the last.

Avoid — Broken

WEP

Wired Equivalent Privacy (1997). The original Wi-Fi security — now completely broken. A hacker with basic tools can crack a WEP password in under a minute. Never use it.

Outdated

WPA

Wi-Fi Protected Access (2003). An emergency replacement for WEP — much better, but still has known weaknesses. You may see it on very old routers.

Current Standard

WPA2

Wi-Fi Protected Access 2 (2004). Uses AES encryption — the same standard governments use. Most routers today use WPA2. A strong password makes it very hard to crack.

Latest & Strongest

WPA3

Wi-Fi Protected Access 3 (2018). Protects even against guessing attacks on weak passwords and secures open (café) networks. Choose this if your router supports it.

Public Wi-Fi warning

Open networks (airports, cafés) have no encryption between your device and the router — anyone on the same network can potentially see your traffic. Always use HTTPS websites or a VPN on public Wi-Fi.

Common Wi-Fi Problems & Fixes

Before calling your ISP, try these — they solve most Wi-Fi problems in under five minutes.

  • Restart the router — unplug it for 30 seconds, plug it back in. Fixes ~80% of issues.
  • Switch to 5 GHz — if you're near the router, 5 GHz is much faster than 2.4 GHz.
  • Reduce interference — move the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, and thick walls.
  • Check for updates — routers have firmware that needs updating occasionally.
  • Check the password — Wi-Fi passwords are case-sensitive.
  • Forget and rejoin — on your device, forget the network and reconnect fresh.
  • Restart the device — Wi-Fi adapters can get stuck.
  • Check if others are affected — if only your device fails, the problem is likely the device not the router.
  • Move the router — place it centrally in your home, raised up, away from walls.
  • Use a Wi-Fi extender — repeats the signal into dead zones. Easy to set up.
  • Try a mesh system — multiple nodes that together blanket your home in one seamless network.
  • Use a powerline adapter — sends data through your home's electrical wiring to a remote room.
  • Run a speed test — visit a speed test site to see if the issue is with your ISP connection or your internal network.
  • Check for heavy users — someone streaming 4K or downloading large files can saturate your connection.
  • Try a wired connection — plug directly into the router with an ethernet cable to isolate whether Wi-Fi is the bottleneck.
  • Contact your ISP — if a wired connection is also slow, there may be an outage or a problem on their end.
